DECLARATION OF MISAEL ALEJANDRO SANCHEZ CUBAS IN SUPPORT OF APPLICATION FOR T NONIMMIGRANT STATUS

I, Misael Alejandro Sanchez Cubas, declare under penalty of perjury that the following statement is true and correct to the best of my knowledge:

1. My name is Misael Alejandro Sanchez Cubas, and I was born on June 13, 1998, in Puebla, Mexico. I am a citizen of Mexico and I currently live in New York. I am writing this declaration to explain what happened to me and why I am seeking T Nonimmigrant Status. In November 2022, a criminal organization forced me into labor trafficking after seizing my business and bringing me to the United States under false promises. I am the father of one daughter, Lucy Victoria Sanchez Vareda, who was born in 2023 in Brooklyn, New York, and is a United States citizen. This declaration describes the trafficking I suffered, how I entered the United States, and why I need protection under the T visa.

## Background Information

2. I grew up in Puebla, Mexico, where my family lived in poverty. My mother raised me along with my older brother and my older sister, but there was never enough money for the things we needed. Since I was a child, I had to work to help my family survive. When I was about sixteen years old, my mother died. After she died, I had to leave school and work full-time to support my family. I finished my high school studies later, around age twenty, because I had to work and study at the same time. When I was eighteen, I started working formally, and my employer registered me with the Mexican social security system.

3. By the time I was in my early twenties, I had saved money and wanted to build my own business. I invested between 300,000 and 400,000 Mexican pesos to start a small clothing manufacturing business. I worked hard, and the business was starting to become stable. Just as things were improving, armed men came to my business and demanded protection money. When I could not pay them, they seized my business and took everything I had built. These armed men threatened my family and made it clear that I could not stay in Puebla safely. Because of the danger they created, I had no choice but to leave Mexico.

## Severe Form of Trafficking in the U.S.

4. My life changed when armed men arrived at my clothing business in Puebla demanding protection money. These men came carrying guns. They threatened to destroy my business and harm my family if I did not do what they said. When I could not pay, they told me I had one option: give them the business, and in exchange, they would give me a job in the United States. They promised the journey would take only three hours of walking, that the job was good and real, and that they would pay for everything from Ciudad Juarez onward. They did not ask me for money. Instead, they took my business — everything I had worked for and saved — as the price. They told me to go to the CAPU bus station in Puebla and buy a ticket to Ciudad Juarez. I did what they said because they had guns and had threatened my family.

5. At the bus station, a member of the organization met me. He told me not to speak to the other people traveling. When I arrived in Ciudad Juarez, they divided the group into fours and held me in a hotel. After that, they took me to an unfinished house in a desert area near the border. I waited there for two or three days. They gave me only eggs and water. Then four or five armed men arrived carrying rifles. They loaded me into the back of a truck and covered me with heavy tarps so I could not see. They drove over rough terrain for a long time. When the armed men pulled off the tarps, they forced me to walk through the desert. I walked for about six hours, and then for four more hours after dark. The journey they had promised would take three hours took over ten hours of exhausting walking through the desert. Before I left the first house in Mexico, the armed men took my phone. They told me I could no longer use it. I had no way to call anyone, no way to contact my family, and no way to ask for help.

6. Border patrol officers caught me near a highway around two in the morning. They held me for four or five hours, then brought me to a bridge and told me to walk back to Mexico. But the same guide who had been leading me through the desert did not ask what I wanted to do. He told me, "Follow me." He found a phone, made a call, and within twenty minutes a car arrived and took me back to the same house. The next day, the same armed men came and forced me to cross again. I went through the same process — covered in tarps, driven over rough terrain, forced to walk for hours. This time I made it across and reached a house near a highway in Texas. I waited there for three or four hours, then they put me in a car and drove me to another house where I waited twelve hours. At four in the morning, the traffickers hid me inside a truck with about ten other people. That truck drove all the way to Albuquerque, New Mexico.

7. At the stash house in Albuquerque, four people were in charge. One of them was the boss. The boss told me, "You are going to help me with some errands." He asked what I had in my pockets. I gave him my wallet, and he took my driver's license and my voter identification card. He told me he was keeping my documents. Because I was the only person in the house with a driver's license, the boss singled me out. He gave me a truck and placed an armed guard next to me as a copilot. The boss forced me to drive other migrants from the house to drop-off points about twenty minutes away, and to pick up new arrivals coming to the house. I did this every single day for about a month. The boss told me that if police stopped me, I should show my license quickly and say I did not know who the passengers were. I asked the boss many times when I would be paid. Every time, he told me to shut up and stop asking. I was never paid anything.

8. Besides the forced driving, the boss forced me to cook all the meals for everyone in the house, clean the bathrooms twice a week, and wash the traffickers' trucks and vehicles almost every day. While I worked constantly — cooking, cleaning, driving, and washing — the boss and his guards sat around smoking, drinking, and using drugs. They did nothing. They gave orders while I served them. The living conditions were terrible. The house was too small. There were no beds and no chairs. I slept sitting down or crouching because there was no room to lie flat. The boss forced me to stand in silence for hours at a time. If I moved or spoke, the guards hit me or cursed at me. One time, when I complained about standing for so long, the guards beat me. After that, I learned to stay silent no matter how much pain I was in.

9. I lived in constant fear because the boss carried a gun at all times. He told me that if I did not stay quiet, he would kill me. The guards also pressured me to smoke and drink with them. If I refused, they screamed at me, degraded me, and called me worthless until I did what they said. I had never used these substances regularly before, and being forced to use them damaged my health. When I finally left that house, I had withdrawal symptoms — my heart raced, my hands shook, and I could not sleep. Every day I asked the boss about the job they had promised me. Every day he told me to shut up and stop asking. I could not escape because the boss had taken my documents, I had no money, no phone, and no idea where I was. The copilot watched me at all times. The boss had made it clear that if I tried to leave, he would find me and hurt me.

10. After about fifteen days, the boss demanded that I give him a family member's phone number. I was so afraid that I gave him my uncle's number in Delaware. The boss called my uncle and lied to him, saying I owed money and that my family had to pay for my release. My family spent the next fifteen days gathering the money. Once they paid, the boss's men drove me to Delaware, where my uncle picked me up. My uncle told me what the traffickers had done — how they called, lied about a debt, and demanded money. I had been held in Albuquerque for approximately one month. During that entire time, the boss forced me to work without pay, beat me, threatened me with a gun, cut me off from everyone, and treated me like a slave. When I finally got into my uncle's car, I felt like I could breathe for the first time since the armed men took my business.

## Physical Presence on Account of Trafficking

11. My physical presence in the United States is directly connected to the trafficking. I would not be in this country if the traffickers had not brought me here through force and false promises. They promised me a job and safe passage. Instead, they held me in Albuquerque, New Mexico, for about one month starting in November 2022 and forced me to work without pay as a driver, cook, and cleaner. I could not leave because they took my documents, kept me isolated, and threatened to kill me. After my family paid a ransom, the traffickers transported me to Delaware, and I then traveled to New York.

12. I have been physically present in the United States continuously since November 2022. I could not leave when the traffickers held me, and I cannot safely return to Mexico because the same criminal organization still operates there. I remain in New York, where I work as a food delivery person and care for my daughter, Lucy Victoria Sanchez Vareda, who was born in Brooklyn in 2023 and is a United States citizen.

## Cooperation with Law Enforcement

13. I have not yet reported the trafficking to law enforcement. At the time, I did not understand that what happened to me was trafficking or that I could report it and receive protection. When border patrol detained me during my first crossing attempt, no one explained to me that I was a victim or that help was available. I am now ready and willing to cooperate fully with any law enforcement investigation into the men who trafficked me. I want to come forward not just for myself, but because I want to help prevent what happened to me from happening to other people. My uncle in Delaware, who received the ransom calls and paid money to the traffickers, is also willing to serve as a witness.

## Extreme Hardship in Mexico

14. Under INA § 101(a)(15)(T)(i)(IV) and 8 C.F.R. § 214.209, I must show that removal to my home country would cause me extreme hardship. If I am forced to return to Mexico, I will face grave danger. The same criminal organization that trafficked me still operates in Puebla. These men took my business, threatened my family, and forced me to work for them. If I return, they will find me and force me to work for them again, or they will kill me. Law enforcement in Mexico does not effectively protect trafficking victims, and criminal organizations in my region operate freely. There is no safety for me in Mexico.

15. My daughter, Lucy Victoria Sanchez Vareda, is a United States citizen born in Brooklyn in 2023. She depends on me for her care and emotional wellbeing. Separating me from Lucy would cause extreme hardship to both of us. She would lose her father during the most important years of her life, and I would lose the ability to care for my only child.

16. I also suffer from anxiety, nightmares, and trauma from the trafficking. I wake up in the middle of the night with my heart racing, remembering the stash house in Albuquerque. I have an untreated arm injury from falling in the desert during the crossing that still causes me pain when I raise my arm. I am planning to begin psychological therapy, but mental health services in Mexico are severely limited and there is widespread stigma against trafficking survivors. Removing me would interrupt my recovery, cut off my access to medical care, and prevent me from cooperating with law enforcement regarding the trafficking.

## Good Moral Character and Waiver of Inadmissibility

17. I acknowledge that I entered the United States without inspection on two occasions in November 2022. The first time, border patrol caught me and returned me to Mexico. The second time, one day later, I entered successfully. Both entries happened under the control of the trafficking organization that had seized my business and threatened my family. I did not enter voluntarily — armed men forced me to leave Mexico and deceived me about what waited for me here. I sincerely apologize for violating immigration law. I also received a traffic ticket in 2024, and I am attending all court dates to resolve this matter lawfully.

18. Since arriving safely in New York, I have worked hard to live honestly and follow the law. I work as a food delivery person because this job allows me to work under my own name. I refuse to use false identity documents because I respect the law and want to build my life the right way. I have paid taxes every year since arriving in New York. I have never stolen from anyone, never robbed or extorted anyone, and my FBI record is clean.

19. I am an active member of a labor rights organization in New York that fights wage theft for undocumented workers and helps people access resources like driver's licenses and food assistance. I can provide a letter from this organization confirming my membership. I take care of my daughter Lucy every day, and I am committed to being a responsible father and a positive member of my community.

## Conclusion

20. I respectfully and humbly ask that you grant me T Nonimmigrant Status in recognition of the trafficking I suffered and the severe harm it has caused me. I declare under penalty of perjury that every statement in this declaration is true and correct. I am committed to healing from my trauma, cooperating with law enforcement, and rebuilding my life with my daughter in safety and peace. Thank you for considering my petition.
