# Trafficking Section Instructions

## Overview
This is the CORE narrative section. It is the largest section (~2,000-3,000 words) and must comprehensively establish all elements of trafficking with clarity, specificity, and legal precision.

---

## GEOGRAPHIC SCOPE RULE

**US EVENTS ONLY.** Trafficking narrative focuses exclusively on what happened in the United States.

**One exception allowed**: A single brief reference to pre-US escalation (e.g., "When we were still in [country], [trafficker] had already begun controlling me by..."). This reference must be minimal and may appear in the Process/Setup paragraph if the pattern directly explains the US exploitation.

**After that**: No more pre-US narrative. Forward momentum to US trafficking.

---

## STRUCTURE & PARAGRAPH PROGRESSION

### FIRST PARAGRAPH = PROCESS/SETUP (MANDATORY)

**Purpose**: Establish HOW the trafficking relationship was set up and WHY victim agreed.

**Required elements:**
- Who the trafficker was (name, relationship to client)
- How client and trafficker connected (met at work, through family, online, etc.)
- What trafficker promised (job, housing, education, relationship stability, better life, salary)
- What job/situation was described (type of work, location, conditions promised)
- Why client agreed (believed the promises, saw opportunity, had no alternative, trusted trafficker, vulnerable circumstances)
- How the "hook" was set — the moment client committed or agreed

**CRITICAL FRAMING RULE**: Frame the agreement around TRAFFICKER'S PROMISES, not victim's desperation.

**WRONG**: "I was so desperate for money that I said yes to anything."
**RIGHT**: "[Trafficker] promised me a job earning $2,000 per month working in a restaurant. He said I could stay in an apartment with other workers and save money to help my family. I trusted him because..."

**ABSOLUTE PROHIBITION**: No exploitation in this paragraph. This is setup only. Exploitation and enforcement belong in subsequent paragraphs.

**Example opening**: "In [month/year], I met [trafficker's name] at [location]. He told me he could get me a job in [city] at [type of work]. He promised I would earn [amount] and could [specific benefit]. Because [reason], I agreed..."

---

### RECRUITMENT PROGRESSION (For Gradual/Relationship-Based Trafficking)

**When applicable**: Some cases involve slow grooming or relationship-building before explicit exploitation.

**Structure option**: Setup may span 2-4 paragraphs showing:
1. Initial meeting and promise
2. Trust-building (gifts, attention, support, reliability)
3. Increasing dependency (financial, emotional, or circumstantial)
4. Exploitation onset (distinct paragraph marking the turning point)

**EXPLOITATION ONSET MARKER**: When moving from setup to actual exploitation, use a DISTINCT SHORT PARAGRAPH to mark the turning point.

**Example transition**: "At first, [trafficker] was kind. [Specific example]. But after [timeframe], everything changed. When I arrived at [location], I realized the job was not what he had promised."

This signals to the reader: here is where the victim understood they had been deceived.

---

### SECOND PARAGRAPH = LABOR + ENFORCEMENT (MANDATORY)

**Purpose**: Establish the WORK and HOW COMPLIANCE WAS SECURED.

**Required elements:**
- Specific tasks (cooking, cleaning, care work, agricultural labor, manufacturing, commercial sex, etc.)
- Hours and frequency (worked 6am-11pm daily, worked every day, worked weekends, etc.)
- Who benefited from the work (trafficker, trafficker's spouse, customers, business owner, etc.)
- HOW compliance was enforced (the first control method that drove work)

**LABOR FORMULA** (Apply to each task type):
Task → Victim's potential resistance → How enforcement prevented resistance

**Example**: "I was required to cook meals for [number] of people every morning starting at 5am. If the food was not ready by [time], or if [trafficker] believed it was too salty or undercooked, he would [enforcement consequence]. This meant I could never rest..."

**Scope boundary**: This paragraph focuses on labor itself and one primary control mechanism. Additional control methods get dedicated paragraphs.

---

### SUBSEQUENT PARAGRAPHS — One Per Control Method (CRITICAL)

**When a paragraph is needed**: Every distinct control method used by trafficker to maintain exploitation gets its own paragraph.

**Distinct control methods** include:
- Debt bondage (financial entrapment)
- Document confiscation (passport/ID control)
- Threats to family/children
- Threats to involve immigration authorities
- Threats to commit violence
- Threats about reporting/getting client "in trouble"
- Isolation (preventing contact with outside world)
- Physical violence or substance abuse coercion
- Sexual abuse (if separate from labor)
- Surveillance (monitoring movements/communications)
- Psychological manipulation/threats to reputation
- Threat of deportation or legal consequences

**Paragraph structure** (5-8+ sentences each):
- Open with situation or control mechanism
- Explain WHAT the control was
- Explain WHY it was effective (what did victim fear?)
- Provide at least one specific example or scene
- Connect to trafficking function (how did it keep victim enslaved?)

---

## CRITICAL FILTER: VAWA-vs-TRAFFICKING

**Self-test every paragraph**: "Does this connect MEANS to ENDS?"

The difference between domestic violence and trafficking is the presence of a MEANS OF CONTROL and a resulting DEPRIVATION OF LIBERTY.

### VAWA Examples (NOT trafficking alone):
- "She insulted me and made me feel small"
- "He was jealous and wouldn't let me go out"
- "He drank and yelled at me"
- "He refused to let me have friends"

### TRAFFICKING Examples (Same facts + connection):
- "She insulted me to keep me obedient. When I refused to cook, she said I was worthless and threatened to tell my family I was a prostitute"
- "He tracked my location with his phone and wouldn't let me go out alone because he said if I escaped, he would call immigration on me"
- "He drank every evening and if I tried to sleep, he would wake me to cook, work, or have sex. Sleep deprivation kept me confused and unable to plan escape"
- "He isolated me from friends by telling them I was a thief and no one should trust me, which meant I had no one to help me escape"

### MEANS-ENDS MANDATORY PAIRING

Every single control mechanism must be explicitly tied to a RESULT (the end it served).

**Formula**: [Control method] → [Specific consequence] → [Result: why victim couldn't escape/resist]

**WRONG**: "He took my passport away."
**RIGHT**: "He took my passport away. Without it, I had no identification, no way to get a job legally, and no way to return to [country]. I was completely dependent on him for survival."

**WRONG**: "He threatened to call immigration."
**RIGHT**: "He threatened to call immigration. He showed me [specific detail about how this threat worked — did he have her address? Did he show her his phone?]. Because I had entered the country [without inspection/on fraudulent visa], if he called, I would be deported immediately and separated from my child. I stayed because I had no choice — the moment I tried to leave, he would destroy everything."

---

## SUBSTANCE/BODY CONTROL — VAWA TRAP (CRITICAL)

Forced substance use, sleep deprivation, and dietary control can be trafficking if they serve a trafficking function. But standalone, they risk appearing as abuse without trafficking.

**REQUIRED**: Connect substance use/sleep deprivation to trafficking purpose.

**WRONG**: "Every day, he forced me to take pills. I became addicted and confused."
**RIGHT**: "Every day, he forced me to take pills. He told me they would 'keep me calm.' Really, they made me confused and unable to plan escape. When I tried to stop taking them, he locked me in a room. The drugs kept me dependent and unable to resist the labor demands or leave."

**WRONG**: "He kept me awake constantly."
**RIGHT**: "He kept me awake for 72 hours at a time, forcing me to work continuously. Sleep deprivation made me unable to think clearly about escape. When I was exhausted, I became compliant — I would do whatever he asked just to be allowed to rest. This was deliberate. Exhaustion was his tool to control me."

---

## COERCED COMPLIANCE — NEVER VOLUNTARY

Every action taken by victim must be fear-driven. No voluntary choices within the trafficking situation.

**WRONG**: "I worked long hours because I wanted to earn money to help my family."
**RIGHT**: "I worked long hours because [trafficker] threatened [specific consequence]. I could not refuse. If I slowed down or complained, he would [enforcement consequence]. I had no choice."

**WRONG**: "I gave him my salary because I trusted him to save it for me."
**RIGHT**: "I gave him my entire salary because if I refused, he threatened to [specific consequence]. He controlled the money — I received only what he decided to give me for necessities, which was rarely enough."

---

## ENFORCEMENT MECHANISM — CRITICAL RULE

Every forced action must specify HOW compliance was secured, with SPECIFIC consequences, not vague references.

**WRONG**: "I had to obey him."
**RIGHT**: "If I disobeyed, he would [specific consequence: hit me, lock me in the basement, threaten to contact immigration, threaten my child, etc.]."

**WRONG**: "I was afraid of what he might do."
**RIGHT**: "I was afraid because [specific previous incident that showed what he was capable of]. When [situation X], he had [specific response]. I knew that if I tried to escape, he would not hesitate to [consequence informed by that previous incident]."

---

## VAGUE-CONSEQUENCE PROHIBITION

Remove all vague threat language. Replace with specificity.

**WRONG**: "I had seen what they did" — to whom? what did they do?
**RIGHT**: "I had seen what they did to [name/role]. When [situation], they [specific action]. I knew they would do the same to me."

**WRONG**: "I did not know what they would do"
**RIGHT**: "I did not know what they would do, but I knew from [specific incident/observation] that they were capable of [consequence]. That was enough to keep me in line."

**WRONG**: "He threatened violence"
**RIGHT**: "He threatened to [specific violent action], and I had already experienced his violence when [specific prior incident]. I believed he would follow through."

---

## FINANCIAL EXPLOITATION (if applicable)

**Trafficker as active agent**: Show trafficker's role in controlling money.

**Required elements:**
- What work victim performed
- What victim was promised as pay
- What victim actually received
- Who kept the rest
- What victim could/could not do with money received

**Wage fraud formula** (when applicable):
- Hours worked × promised hourly rate = what victim should have earned
- Actual pay received = what victim got
- Difference = stolen wages

**Example**: "I worked 12 hours per day, 6 days per week. [Trafficker] promised $1,200 per month. I should have earned approximately $7,200 monthly (12 hours × $50/hour rate × 12 days worked per month). Instead, he gave me only $200 per month for personal expenses, and kept the rest, claiming he was saving it for me or using it to pay my debt."

**Active control language**:
- "He kept my paychecks"
- "He took all my earnings"
- "He controlled my bank account"
- "He decided how much I was allowed to spend"
- NOT: "My money was never available to me" (passive)

---

## SERVITUDE CONTRAST

For all forced labor cases, contrast trafficker's idleness with victim's work.

**Why**: Establishes the unreasonable and coercive nature of the labor arrangement.

**Example**: "While I worked 14-hour days cooking, cleaning, and caring for the children, [trafficker] sat in the living room watching television or napping. She spent the money I earned on herself — clothes, jewelry, dining out — while I was given leftovers to eat and had no money for my own needs."

**Effect**: Makes clear this is not a normal employment or family relationship, but servitude.

---

## LABOR PARAGRAPH GRANULARITY

If victim performed multiple labor types or if labor was enforced through multiple distinct mechanisms, separate into distinct paragraphs.

**Example structure**:
- Paragraph A: Domestic labor (cooking, cleaning, childcare) + enforcement method 1
- Paragraph B: Additional labor (agricultural work, factory work) + enforcement method 2
- Paragraph C: Financial control and debt bondage + enforcement method 3
- Paragraph D: Sexual abuse (if separate from labor trade)

**Principle**: Granularity makes the narrative clearer and allows adequate development of each control method.

---

## SURVEILLANCE AS TRAFFICKING TOOL

When surveillance was used, frame it as a LABOR/SERVICE CONTROL mechanism, not as jealousy.

**WRONG**: "He was jealous and tracked my location constantly."
**RIGHT**: "He installed a tracking app on my phone without my knowledge. Whenever I left the house, he would call or text demanding to know where I was, who I was with, and when I would return. This surveillance was his way of ensuring I went only where he authorized — to work, to run his errands, and nowhere else. If I deviated even slightly from the route he expected, he would accuse me of plotting to escape. The tracking was part of how he kept me under control."

---

## CONTROL MECHANISM ESTABLISHMENT

For each control method, explain HOW it was initially established.

**Examples**:
- How did trafficker confiscate documents? ("I arrived at [location] and he told me to give him my passport. He said he needed it for [false reason]. I gave it to him. He never returned it.")
- How did debt bondage begin? ("He told me [false information about expenses]. He said I owed him [amount] before I could leave. Every month he added more charges...")
- How was victim isolated? ("He told me I could not call my family because [reason]. He took my phone the first night and never gave it back...")

---

## PARAGRAPH OPENING VARIETY

Vary sentence openings to create natural narrative flow and avoid monotony.

**Acceptable**: Max 2-3 paragraphs may open with trafficker as the first-clause subject.
**Vary with**: Victim experience, victim emotion, victim perspective, pattern descriptions, scene-grounding.

**Example varied openings**:
- "Over the following months, [trafficker] expanded his control..."
- "I soon realized that [description]..."
- "Every single day, I..."
- "The situation escalated when [incident]..."
- "[Trafficker] made clear that..."
- "I had no way to [resist/escape/communicate] because..."
- "What started as promises became threats when..."

---

## FIRING FEAR PROHIBITION

**HARD FAIL** if narrative includes "afraid they would fire me" or "worried about losing the job" as a control mechanism.

**Why**: This is not trafficking — it's normal employment risk. Trafficking requires UNLAWFUL control, not employment termination.

**If relevant**: Reframe to show the UNLAWFUL control beneath the employment framing.

**WRONG**: "I was afraid he would fire me if I didn't work harder."
**RIGHT**: "If I worked slowly or complained, he would [unlawful consequence: lock me in a room, take my phone, threaten to deport me, refuse to feed me, etc.]. The job itself was not the issue — the issue was the unlawful control he exercised over me through [the actual trafficking means]."

---

## THREAT RULES

### Threat Presentation
**Default format**: Direct quoted speech when possible.

**Example**: "[Trafficker] told me, 'If you try to leave, I will call immigration and you will be deported. You will never see your children again.' He said this repeatedly, especially when I showed any sign of resistance."

### Transmission Specificity
Every threat needs context:
- **WHO made it?** (trafficker, trafficker's associate, third party acting on trafficker's behalf)
- **WHEN was it made?** (in response to what? during what situation?)
- **WHAT exact words?** (quoted when possible; paraphrased with specificity when necessary)

**WRONG**: "He threatened to call immigration."
**RIGHT**: "[Trafficker] told me, 'If you run away, I will call immigration. The police will come and take you. You will be locked in a detention center and your children will be taken from you.' He repeated this threat every time I mentioned wanting to contact my family."

### Impact Elaboration
After stating threat, explain why it was effective.

**Formula**: Threat → Why victim believed it → Why victim could not resist

**Example**: "[Trafficker] told me he had my social security number and knew where my family lived. He had actually walked me past my mother's house once, showing me he could find her. I believed he meant it. If I tried to escape, he could destroy my family. That thought kept me obedient."

---

## INSULT SPECIFICITY

When psychological control was used, provide the actual insults or describe their nature.

**WRONG**: "He insulted me constantly."
**RIGHT**: "He called me 'worthless,' 'stupid,' 'a whore,' 'a thief.' He told me my own family didn't want me and that no one would ever believe my story. He said no one would hire me if I left him. These insults were constant — every day he found something to criticize about my appearance, my work, my accent, my family. Over time, I began to believe him. I thought I was worthless and that I deserved this life."

**Why specificity matters**: Shows the systematic nature of psychological control and how it served to maintain compliance.

---

## ESCAPE BARRIERS — EXPLICIT EXPLANATION (CRITICAL)

For each barrier to escape, explain WHY victim could not overcome it.

**WRONG**: "I couldn't escape."
**RIGHT**: "[Trafficker] had my passport. I had no money. He had threatened to call immigration if I tried to leave. My English was limited and I didn't know how to get help. I didn't know the name of the city I was in. I had no friends or family nearby. Even if I had run away, where would I go? Who would help me? And he had made clear that he would hunt me down and make me pay for leaving."

**Barriers to address** (if applicable):
- Document control
- Financial control
- Threats to family/children
- Threats of legal consequences
- Language barriers
- Isolation from community
- Distrust of authorities
- Prior negative experience with authorities
- Psychological belief that escape is impossible
- Not knowing where to go or who to call
- Fear of consequences if caught

---

## CHILD SEPARATION (if applicable)

If client had a child but was unable to stay with them due to trafficking:

**Required**: Explain that parent wanted to be with child but was prevented by trafficking.

**Example**: "I have a [age]-year-old daughter, [name]. When [trafficker] first recruited me, I was promised I could bring her with me or that I would be able to visit her. Instead, when I arrived, [trafficker] made clear I would not be permitted to contact her or anyone who could care for her. I wanted to be a mother to her. That desire to protect her and stay in her life is part of what kept me trapped. I feared that if I tried to escape, [trafficker] would find her and harm her, or that I would be deported and separated from her permanently. The trafficking took my motherhood from me."

---

## ISOLATION INTEGRITY — CRITICAL RULE

Do NOT include details that prove victim had outside support, maintained dual personas, or had successful outside contact. Such details undermine trafficking credibility.

**HARD FAIL examples**:
- "My American friends told me to leave" (victim had outside support network)
- "My family called every week and I lied about my situation" (victim maintained dual persona and family contact)
- "I sometimes went to the store alone" (victim had unsupervised movement)
- "I managed to sneak a phone call to a counselor" (victim had resources and communication ability)

**Safe examples**:
- "I was never allowed to use the phone"
- "Every time I left the house, [trafficker] or a family member accompanied me"
- "I had no contact with anyone outside the household"
- "I was never given the opportunity to speak to anyone privately"

**Principle**: Isolation is a trafficking means. If you claim it, do not simultaneously show successful escape of isolation. If victim had outside contact, the trafficking narrative must show why that contact did not lead to escape (fear, family in country of origin being threatened, etc.).

---

## SCENE-GROUNDING (CRITICAL)

Abstract control mechanisms are less credible than grounded scenes. For EVERY MAJOR CONTROL METHOD, provide at least one specific trigger scene.

**Formula**: Specific situation → What victim did/said → How trafficker responded → What victim learned about consequences

**Example scene** (for document control):
"Two months after I arrived, I asked [trafficker] for my passport. I wanted to look at it because my son's birthday was coming and I wanted to write to him. [Trafficker] became angry. He said, 'I am keeping this to protect you. If you try to take it, I will call the police and tell them you stole money from me. You will go to jail and your children will forget about you.' He locked the passport in a drawer and kept the key on his person. After that incident, I never asked for it again. I understood I had no right to my own documents."

**Why scenes matter**: They make the trafficking real and tangible. They show the moment victim understood the depth of control.

---

## CONVERSATIONAL AUTHENTICITY

Include 3-5 natural speech markers that reflect genuine witness testimony.

**Examples of authentic markers**:
- "I remember the day..."
- "I can still see..."
- "To this day, I cannot..."
- "The first time he [action], I thought..."
- "What hurt the most was..."
- "I realized then that..."
- "Even now, when I think about..."
- "At the time, I did not understand..."

**Principle**: These markers create authenticity. They signal this is a genuine memory being recounted, not a legal document being composed.

**Caution**: Use sparingly and authentically. 3-5 markers across the entire trafficking section is appropriate. Overuse becomes artificial.

---

## TRAFFICKING AFTERMATH PARAGRAPH

Include a dedicated paragraph describing the financial and practical aftermath of trafficking.

**Required elements**:
- When/how victim escaped or was released
- What happened to victim's earnings
- Victim's financial situation after trafficking ended
- Any debts or financial obligations imposed by trafficker that still linger
- Practical consequences (homelessness, inability to work, etc.)

**Example**: "When I finally escaped in [month/year], I had nothing. [Trafficker] had stolen all my wages — approximately [amount]. I had no money, no documentation, no housing. I stayed with [person/shelter] for several weeks. To this day, I am trying to rebuild my life. I am living [description of current situation]. My debts from the trafficking remain [outstanding/partially paid]. The financial trauma of trafficking continues to affect my ability to achieve stability."

**Purpose**: Shows trafficking's ongoing harms and why victim needs immigration relief and protections.

---

## NARRATIVE CLOSURE

End the trafficking section with the moment of escape/freedom/release.

**Example**: "In [month/year], while [trafficker] was [away/distracted/specific circumstance], I finally found the courage to run. I took only the clothes I was wearing and [brief detail about how escape occurred — did someone help? Did victim just run? Was there a specific catalyst?]. I was terrified the entire time. I kept expecting [trafficker] to catch me and bring me back. It was only when I reached [location/person] that I began to believe I might be free."

**Why ending with escape matters**: It transitions victim from captivity to freedom, setting stage for why law enforcement cooperation and T visa protection are now possible.

---

## COMMERCIAL SEX / DEBT BONDAGE (if applicable)

If trafficking involved sexual exploitation, dedicate a paragraph or two to this.

**For sexual trafficking**:
- How victim was recruited into sexual trade
- Where sexual services were sold (brothel, street, online, private residence, etc.)
- Who profited (trafficker, pimp, business owner, etc.)
- How many clients per day/week
- What protection victim had (none)
- What happened to earnings
- How victim was prevented from leaving

**For debt bondage**:
- What "debt" was client told they owed
- How much was claimed at start
- How much was it supposed to be paid down per work period
- What happened to the debt (increased, remained static, moved goalposts)
- When client realized they would never repay it

**Example** (sexual trafficking): "Over the next three months, I was forced to have sex with [number] to [number] men per day. [Trafficker] rented out a small room in [location]. Men would come and pay [trafficker] for [description]. I received nothing. [Trafficker] kept all the money. If I refused, or if I tried to hide money, he would [enforcement consequence]. I became pregnant during this period. I miscarried from the trauma and the physical demands of being forced to have sex continuously. [Trafficker] never took me to a doctor."

---

## LENGTH & DENSITY

Trafficking section typically spans 5-10 pages in completed declaration (depending on case complexity).

**Word count**: 2,000-3,000 words typical.

**Granularity**: More specific and detailed cases warrant more paragraphs and pages.

**Balance**: Sufficient detail to establish all trafficking elements clearly; not so dense that reader loses track of narrative.

---

## SUMMARY OF CRITICAL RULES

1. **US EVENTS ONLY** — one brief pre-US reference allowed
2. **FIRST PARAGRAPH = PROCESS/SETUP** — promises not desperation
3. **SUBSEQUENT PARAGRAPHS = ONE CONTROL METHOD EACH** — 5-8+ sentences, means tied to ends
4. **VAWA FILTER** — every paragraph connects means to ends
5. **SPECIFIC CONSEQUENCES** — no vague threats
6. **SCENE GROUNDING** — at least one scene per major control method
7. **HARD FAILS**: Firing fear, "I don't know what they would do," vague references, isolation integrity broken
8. **FORCED COMPLIANCE** — every action fear-driven
9. **FINANCIAL DETAIL** — amounts, formulas, active trafficker agency
10. **NARRATIVE CLOSURE** — end with escape/freedom
