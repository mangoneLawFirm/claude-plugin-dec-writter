# Extreme Hardship Section Instructions

## Format Rules
- **Paragraph count**: 3 paragraphs maximum (4 paragraphs only if extensive medical/psychological issues requiring detailed explanation)
- **Word count**: 250-400 words typical (HARD FAIL if exceeds 500)
- **Numbering**: Continue sequential numbering from Law Enforcement section
- **Heading**: No section heading

## Mandatory Opening Requirement

**Every EH declaration must open with legal standard citation.**

Standard language to include:
"To remain in the United States, I am required to demonstrate that my removal or departure would result in extreme hardship to myself or to my qualifying family members. As established in the Immigration and Nationality Act and applicable regulations, extreme hardship is defined as [brief explanation of what constitutes hardship beyond what ordinarily accompanies removal]."

**Alternatively** (more concise):
"The law requires me to show that returning to [country of origin] would cause extreme hardship to me and my family. I submit the following circumstances that demonstrate such hardship:"

---

## Three-Paragraph Structure (Standard)

### Paragraph 1: Safety + Country Conditions (MERGED)

**Combine** personal safety concerns AND country conditions into ONE paragraph. Do NOT separate these.

**Required elements**:
- **Personal safety threat**: What specific threat or condition makes return unsafe for client
- **Country conditions context**: General conditions in country of origin that compound the threat
- **Why return is dangerous**: Clear causal connection

**Example themes**:
- Trafficker still active and at large in country of origin; country has no protection for trafficking victims; victim would be at risk of re-trafficking
- Witness to crime; trafficker has family/associates in country of origin; victim would be targeted for retaliation
- Political persecution, gang violence, or generalized violence in country that endangers client
- Medical condition that cannot be safely treated in country; healthcare system inadequate
- Gender-based violence in country makes return dangerous for women

**Critical rule**: Country conditions are NOT standalone. They must be connected to client's specific vulnerability.

**WRONG**: "My country has gang violence and poverty" (generic country info)
**RIGHT**: "My country is controlled by gangs. [Trafficker] has ties to [gang/criminal organization] in [region]. If I return, I would be at risk of re-trafficking or retaliation. The country's law enforcement cannot protect trafficking victims — most cases go unprosecuted. Women who report trafficking are stigmatized and unsafe."

**Scene grounding**: Include at least one specific detail about why return is dangerous.
- "When [trafficking] first occurred, [trafficker] made clear he had connections in [country]. He said, '[If you try to leave, I will find you when you go back]. I have family everywhere.'"
- "The region of [country] where my family lives is controlled by the gang [gang name]. [Trafficker] is affiliated with them. I have been told by [person] that [trafficker] has sworn to find me if I return."

### Paragraph 2: Family Separation + Support System Loss

**Required elements**:
- **Family in US**: Name, relationship, ages of US-based family members (spouse, children, parents who are US citizens/LPRs)
- **Separation hardship**: Why separation from this family would be extreme
- **Support system dependency**: Client's emotional, financial, or practical dependency on US-based family
- **Children's situation**: If client has US-citizen or LPR children, their ties to US (school, siblings, stability) and impact of removal on them
- **Caregiver role** (if applicable): Client's role providing care to US-based family members

**Critical distinction**:
- **Client's hardship**: Client would be separated from family support system
- **Family's hardship**: Family (especially US citizens) would be separated from client, lose financial/emotional support, face disruption

**Example structure**: "I have a [relationship] in the United States — [name], a [US citizen/LPR]. We are very close and [specific example of bond: I help support them financially / They helped me escape trafficking / We live together / etc.]. My removal would cause extreme hardship to [family member] because [specific consequence: they depend on my income / they would lose my emotional support / my children would be separated from their other parent]. Additionally, [if applicable: my children are US citizens/LPRs who have lived their entire lives in the US. They attend school here, they have built their lives here. Removing them would uproot their lives and separate them from their father/other family members who are here]."

**Elements to establish**:
- Family member's US status (citizen, LPR, permanent resident)
- Strength of relationship (not just biological)
- How client supports family (financially, emotionally, practically)
- How family supports client (recovery from trafficking, housing, childcare, etc.)
- Children's US ties (birth, schooling, friendships, stability)
- Impact on children if removed (separation from schools, friends, siblings, other parent)

### Paragraph 3: Medical/Therapeutic + Legal Process

**Required elements**:
- **Ongoing medical needs**: Any medical conditions arising from trafficking that require US-based treatment
- **Mental health/PTSD treatment**: Therapy, counseling, psychiatric care specifically for trafficking trauma
- **Lack of treatment access in country of origin**: Brief explanation of why treatment unavailable in home country
- **Legal process timeline**: Current immigration proceedings, T visa application status, ongoing cooperation with law enforcement

**Why this matters**: Demonstrates that client is embedded in US processes (legal, medical) and removal would disrupt critical recovery from trafficking.

**Example structure**: "Since escaping trafficking, I have been receiving treatment for PTSD and trauma from [organization/provider]. My therapist has indicated that my recovery from trafficking trauma is ongoing and requires continued care. Additionally, I am currently cooperating with [law enforcement agency] in their investigation of [trafficker]. My legal immigration status is pending. Removal at this stage would interrupt both my medical recovery and the legal process designed to protect trafficking victims like me."

**Medical details to include** (if applicable):
- What condition(s) client has (PTSD, depression, anxiety, physical injuries requiring ongoing care, etc.)
- What treatment client is receiving (therapy, medication, specialized trauma counseling)
- Whether treatment is available in country of origin (usually not, or of much lower quality)
- How long treatment is expected to continue
- Impact if treatment is interrupted

**Legal process to include**:
- T visa application pending
- Cooperation ongoing with FBI or other LEA
- Potential prosecution of trafficker (client may be needed as witness)
- Immigration court proceedings

## Four-Paragraph Structure (Extended Medical Cases Only)

**Use only if client has extensive medical/psychological needs that cannot be adequately covered in one paragraph.**

**Structure**:
- ¶1: Safety + country conditions
- ¶2: Family separation + support system
- ¶3: Mental health trauma treatment
- ¶4: Medical conditions + legal process

**Paragraph 3 (if separated)**: Focus on PTSD/trauma-specific treatment, therapy, ongoing counseling, recovery stage.

**Paragraph 4 (if separated)**: Physical medical conditions, chronic care needs, medication access, legal process, law enforcement cooperation.

## Critical Rules

### Country Conditions REQUIRED
EH sections MUST include country-specific information. This is where country fears belong (not in Physical Presence section).

**Required**: At least one sentence connecting client's personal vulnerability to the general conditions in country of origin.

**Examples**:
- "In my country, there is widespread corruption and trafficking is not prosecuted effectively..."
- "My country does not have resources for mental health treatment, and facilities for PTSD treatment are non-existent..."
- "In the region where my family lives, gang violence is rampant and law enforcement does not protect trafficking victims..."

### Forward-Looking Only — No Re-narration

Extreme Hardship is NOT a place to re-tell trafficking. It focuses on consequences and future risks, not past events.

**WRONG**: "When I was trafficked, [trafficking details]... This is why returning would be hard."
**RIGHT**: "As a result of trafficking, I have PTSD and cannot work. I depend on my family for support. Returning would cause extreme hardship because I would lose that support while still in recovery."

### Safety Fears + Country Conditions in ONE Paragraph

Do NOT dedicate one paragraph to personal safety and another to country conditions. Merge them into Paragraph 1.

**WRONG**: Separate paragraphs for "Why I fear retaliation" and "Country conditions in my country"
**RIGHT**: "I fear retaliation from [trafficker/gang], and this fear is compounded by the fact that my country has inadequate law enforcement and high rates of [relevant crime]. Traffickers operate with impunity in my region."

## Content Boundaries

### DO INCLUDE:
- Safety threats specific to client
- Country conditions that compound threat
- US family members and dependencies
- Children's US ties
- Ongoing medical/psychological treatment
- Lack of treatment access in home country
- Legal process and law enforcement cooperation
- Emotional bonds with US family
- Financial dependencies

### DO NOT INCLUDE:
- Life built in US, community ties, friendships (belong in Extreme Hardship for LPRs, not T visa applicants)
- Economic hardship alone (must be combined with safety or family)
- Vague fears without grounding
- Re-narration of trafficking events
- Detailed medical records or diagnosis specifics
- Immigration status discussion beyond what's necessary
- Future aspirations or dreams

## Specific Language Examples

### Safety + Country Conditions (¶1)
"If I return to [country], I would be in grave danger. [Trafficker] still lives in [location] and has made clear he views me as a threat because I escaped. My country has not prosecuted [trafficker], and the police are corrupt. Trafficking victims in my country face stigma and are not protected by law enforcement. The region where my family lives is controlled by criminal organizations, and [trafficker] has ties to these groups. There is no safety for me if I return."

### Family Separation (¶2)
"My [relationship], [name], is a US citizen. Since my escape from trafficking, [he/she] has been my primary support. I live with [him/her] and depend on [his/her] financial support while I am recovering from trauma and unable to work full-time. [He/She] depends on me for [specific support: childcare / help with rent / emotional support]. My removal would cause extreme hardship to [him/her] because [explain impact]. Additionally, if I have children, [my US-citizen/LPR children] have spent their entire lives in the United States. They attend school here, they speak English, they have friendships, and they have their stability here. Separating them from their [other parent/extended family] would be devastating."

### Medical/Legal (¶3 or ¶4)
"I am currently receiving treatment for PTSD from my trafficking trauma. My therapist at [organization] has advised that my recovery is ongoing and will take months of continued care. I am also cooperating with the Federal Bureau of Investigation in their investigation of [trafficker]. My T visa petition is pending, and my legal status is uncertain. If I am removed now, I would lose both my medical support and my opportunity to receive T visa protections. The trauma of return combined with loss of treatment would cause severe psychological hardship."

## Word Count Guidance

- **250-300 words**: Straightforward cases with family and safety concerns; standard medical treatment
- **300-350 words**: Cases with significant family separation, multiple US dependents, or moderate medical needs
- **350-400 words**: Complex family situations, extensive mental health needs, or multiple forms of hardship
- **Over 400**: Reduce. Consolidate. Save detailed medical for separate documentation. Only go to 500 in rare cases of severe, extensively documented medical conditions.
