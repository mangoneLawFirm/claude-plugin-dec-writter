# Writing Rules for T-Visa Declaration

These are the CORE rules that apply to EVERY section of the declaration. Section-specific instructions are in `references/`.

## VOICE RULES

### First Person, Client Speaking
The entire declaration uses first person ("I," "me," "my") with the client as the speaker. This centers the victim's perspective and narrative authority.

### Reading Level: 6th–8th Grade
Write for clarity and accessibility. Avoid complex sentence structures, technical jargon (except mandatory legal terms), and multi-clause constructions.

### Emotionally Authentic
The declaration reflects real human experience: confusion, fear, shame, determination to escape. Don't sanitize or over-explain. Let the facts speak.

### NEVER Second Person
Do not address the reader or use "you." No phrases like "as you can see" or "you might wonder."

### FIRST-PERSON SINGULAR ONLY — "I/Me" Exclusively
Use only "I" and "me" when speaking from the victim's perspective. NEVER use "us," "we," or "our" to refer to the victim group or other trafficking victims.

**Exception:** Family "we" is permitted when the victim and family members act as a unit (e.g., "We decided to move to Charlotte together").

**WRONG:** "Everyone in that kitchen knew that if we complained, he would make our lives harder."

**RIGHT:** "Genaro told me that if I complained, he would report me to immigration."

---

## ENGLISH-ONLY — MANDATORY

The entire declaration is written in English. All quotes from Spanish-language speakers are translated into English. No Spanish words, phrases, exclamations, or parenthetical language.

**WRONG:** He would shout, "¡Ya, ya, ya!" at me whenever I was slow.

**RIGHT:** He would shout, "Enough, enough, enough!" at me whenever I was slow.

**WRONG:** I worked in the kitchen using the kind called peroles to cook for large groups.

**RIGHT:** I worked in the kitchen using large metal pots to cook for the family's restaurant business.

---

## ACTIVE VOICE — MANDATORY

The trafficker (or responsible party) must be the grammatical subject of action verbs. Passive voice obscures the trafficker's agency and is not acceptable.

**NEVER:** "I was required to cook for eight hours a day."

**ALWAYS:** "[Name] forced me to cook for eight hours a day."

**NEVER:** "I was taken to Charlotte by van."

**ALWAYS:** "[Name] took me to Charlotte by van."

### HIGH-FREQUENCY VIOLATION ZONE: Journey and Process Narration
This is where passive voice sneaks in most often. Watch for "was told," "was brought," "was given," "was locked in."

**WRONG:** "I was told to leave the house before dawn."

**RIGHT:** "[Name] told me to leave the house before dawn."

**WRONG:** "I was brought to work at the restaurant."

**RIGHT:** "[Name] brought me to work at the restaurant."

---

## PLAIN LANGUAGE

Use simple, direct phrasing. Avoid idioms, colloquialisms, and expressions that sound translated or Spanglish-influenced.

**WRONG:** "To get me in the door, he promised I would earn good money."

**RIGHT:** "He told me I would earn good money to convince me to start working there."

---

## PROHIBITED FRAMING

### Never Frame Around Victim Choice or Desperation
Do not suggest the victim voluntarily agreed or worked out of desperation. This undermines the trafficking narrative.

**WRONG:** "I was afraid they would fire me if I didn't work weekends."

*This implies a voluntary employment relationship and fear of losing a job, not trafficking.*

**RIGHT:** "[Name] said I could not leave and that I would be deported if I refused to work."

**WRONG:** "I needed the work because I had no money."

*This frames the cause as economic desperation, not the trafficker's false promises.*

**RIGHT:** "[Name] promised me I would earn $500 a week if I came to work for his restaurant. He said I would pay him back the cost of my transportation."

### Never Use "Us/We/Our" for the Victim Group
This collective framing obscures individual responsibility and trafficking dynamics.

**WRONG:** "Us workers in the kitchen all knew he was stealing from us."

**RIGHT:** "I knew Genaro was stealing my wages because he never paid me and told me I still owed him money for bringing me here."

## ALLOWED LEGAL TERMS

These terms are correct and required in certain contexts:

- trafficking / human trafficking
- T-Visa / T Nonimmigrant Status
- force / fraud / coercion
- law enforcement
- extreme hardship
- INA (Immigration and Nationality Act)
- C.F.R. (Code of Federal Regulations)
- declaration (of the victim)

## PARAGRAPH STRUCTURE RULES

### Length and Focus
Each paragraph should contain **3–8 sentences** (shorter paragraphs, like transitions, may be 1–2 sentences). One paragraph = one cohesive idea or event.

### Format
- No bullets, dashes, numbered lists, or sub-lists within paragraphs.
- Write in prose form only.
- All information flows as narrative sentences.

### Specificity Requirement
Be SPECIFIC: include names, amounts, dates, times, and concrete actions.

**WRONG:** "He paid me very little."

**RIGHT:** "Genaro paid me $40 a week, even though he promised $500 a week when I arrived."

---

## FORWARD REFERENCE PROHIBITION

Do not use "the [X]" or characterizations like "constant," "ongoing," or "systematic" to refer to something not yet concretely introduced in the text.

**WRONG:** "The abuse started immediately when I arrived. He would hit me every morning."

*"The abuse" assumes the reader already knows about abuse.*

**RIGHT:** "When I arrived at the house, Genaro hit me. He hit me every morning before I started cooking."

First establish facts concretely. Then, if necessary, characterize the pattern.

---

## CONCISENESS

- State each fact once. Do not restate the same point from multiple angles.
- No argumentative analysis (e.g., "This shows he was controlling").
- No retrospective legal framing (e.g., "Which clearly constitutes trafficking").
- No pattern labeling added by the writer (e.g., "This pattern of abuse demonstrates coercion").
- Let facts speak for themselves.

### Total Length
The complete declaration should be **2,500–3,500 words**. Anything over 4,000 words is a FAIL and must be cut.

---

## FACT GROUNDING

Every claim in the declaration must come from the provided facts. Do not invent details, infer outcomes, or elaborate beyond what is documented.

- If a critical fact is missing, use the placeholder **[MISSING FACT: brief description]** exactly and flag it for the interviewer/documentarian.
- Do not fill gaps with assumptions.
- Do not add embellishment.

Before submitting any section, verify:

- [ ] First person ("I/me") only; no "we/us/our" (except family "we")
- [ ] 6th–8th grade reading level
- [ ] All verbs in active voice; no passive ("was forced" → "[Name] forced")
- [ ] No prohibited words; use approved replacements
- [ ] No Spanish words, phrases, or untranslated quotes
- [ ] Specific names, dates, amounts, times
- [ ] Paragraphs 3–8 sentences, one idea per paragraph
- [ ] No forward references without established facts
- [ ] No argumentative framing or legal analysis
- [ ] No bullets or lists; prose only
- [ ] All facts from provided documentation; placeholders for gaps
- [ ] Total declaration on track for 2,500–3,500 words
