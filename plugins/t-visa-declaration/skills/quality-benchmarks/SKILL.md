# T-Visa Declaration Quality Benchmarks

## HARD FAILS — Never Allow These in the Final Declaration

| Rule | Prohibited | Correct |
|------|-----------|---------|
| First-person singular | "us/we/our" for victim group | "I/me/my" exclusively |
| Firing fear | "afraid they would fire me" | Fear of immigration/harm/destitution |
| English-only | ANY Spanish words, quotes, or phrases | All quotes translated to English |
| Sexual violence language | "sexually abused," "sexual assault," "sexual abuse" | "sexual slave," "sexual servitude," "forced sex" |
| VAWA filter | Trafficking para without Means→Ends connection | Every para connects force/fraud to labor/servitude |
| Isolation integrity | Third-party exit advice, family visits proving contact, dual-persona details | Exclude or reframe as control mechanisms |

## Section Word Count Targets

| Section | Target | FAIL if |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Physical Presence | 150-300 words, 1-2 paragraphs | >350 words or 3+ paragraphs |
| Extreme Hardship | 250-400 words, 3 paragraphs max | >500 words or 5+ paragraphs |
| Trafficking | 1,200-2,200 words | Shorter than any other section |
| Total Declaration | 2,500-3,500 words | >4,000 words |
| Background | STRICTLY 2-3 paragraphs | No 3rd paragraph without attorney override |

## Trafficking Section Requirements

**Recruitment Progression (Setup Phase):**
- For gradual trafficking (relationship predated exploitation): setup spans 2-4 paragraphs showing meeting → trust building → dependence → exploitation onset
- For immediate-captivity cases: setup may remain 1 paragraph
- First setup paragraph: who the trafficker was, how met, initial contact
- WHY victim agreed — framed around PROMISES, not victim's needs/desperation
- Setup phase does NOT describe exploitation — that's for later paragraphs
- Setup phase ENDS with an Exploitation Onset Marker: a distinct short paragraph identifying the turning point (triggering change, power shift, new demands)

**Every Trafficking Paragraph Must:**
- Connect MEANS (force/fraud/coercion) to ENDS (labor/servitude/financial exploitation)
- Show HOW control was established (mechanism, not just "forced me")
- Show HOW compliance was enforced (specific threats/consequences)
- Name the trafficker as active grammatical subject
- Pass ISOLATION INTEGRITY check: no detail that proves victim had outside support/contact (third-party exit warnings, family visits, dual-persona descriptions)

**Labor Paragraph Granularity:**
- Domestic servitude / forced labor must be broken into SEPARATE focused paragraphs (cooking ¶, cleaning ¶, enforcement/resistance ¶, financial demands ¶)
- Each labor paragraph must include procedural detail (specific tasks, items, sequences) + its own enforcement mechanism
- Do NOT pack all labor into 1-2 dense paragraphs

**Trafficking Aftermath Paragraph:**
- If trafficking resulted in financial/practical consequences, include a dedicated CLOSING paragraph
- Must state concrete losses (amounts, items, duration) connected to trafficker's actions
- Separate from the final incident/escape paragraph

**Paragraph Opening Variety:**
Max 2-3 trafficking paragraphs may have the trafficker as subject of the FIRST MAIN CLAUSE (even if preceded by brief prep phrases like "After I moved in, [Name]..."). Remaining paragraphs must open with victim experience, emotion, time/setting, or pattern.

## Required Elements

**Physical Presence must include:**
- "directly connected to the trafficking" (explicit causal statement)
- HOW trafficking kept client in US (control, fear, documents, dependency)
- WHY client didn't leave (connected to trafficking, not personal preference)
- Explicit cause-and-effect language: "Because of the trafficking...", "As a result of..."

**Extreme Hardship must include:**
- Statutory language: "extreme hardship involving unusual and severe harm"
- Citation: INA § 101(a)(15)(T)(i)(IV) and 8 C.F.R. § 214.209
- Country conditions paragraph (dedicated, required — not optional)
- Safety fears + country conditions merged into ONE paragraph

**Trafficking must include:**
- EMP mapping: Ends (labor performed) + Means (how forced) + Process (recruit/harbor/obtain)
- Direct quotes for threats when available (in English)
- Escape barrier explanation in isolation/confinement paragraphs
- Wage exploitation formula when pay fraud involved: hours worked × promised rate vs actual pay

## Prohibited Words — Always Substitute

subjected to → forced to | exploitation → being used | experienced trauma → felt scared
psychological abuse → threats | perpetrator → person's name | utilized → used
coercive tactics → threats | confined to premises → not allowed to leave
sustained injuries → was hurt | endured hardship → suffered | commenced → started
subsequent to → after | prior to → before | residing at → living at | pursuant to → because of
aforementioned → the/this | subsequently → later | witnessed → saw | encountered → met
facilitated → helped | on numerous occasions → many times | due to the fact that → because
confiscated → took | compelled to → forced to | smuggling → NEVER USE
respectfully request (standalone) → respectfully and humbly ask

## Good vs Bad Examples

**Trafficking Detail:**
- BAD: "She forced me to cook lunch and dinner, and she also made me clean the house."
- GOOD: "Most days, I came home late and tired, and I still had to cook. I would make dinner from scratch — rice, beans, meat or chicken, tortillas, and eggs, depending on what we had. I also had to prepare food for the next day, pack leftovers, wash dishes, clean the kitchen, take out the trash, and leave everything spotless, because if she saw a mess, she would start yelling."

**VAWA vs Trafficking:**
- BAD (VAWA): "She insulted me and made me feel small, like nothing I did was enough. I would send my location because I was trying to avoid accusations."
- GOOD (Trafficking): "She insulted me constantly to keep me obedient. If I showed any resistance to cooking or cleaning after my shift, she would threaten to call immigration. She demanded I send my location to verify I was at work earning the money she required."

**Coerced Compliance:**
- BAD (voluntary): "I would do those chores fast and right, because my goal was to keep the peace."
- GOOD (coerced): "I did those chores as fast as I could because I was terrified of what she would do if she found anything wrong. She had threatened to call police and have me deported, and I believed she would do it."

**Conclusion Tone:**
- BAD: "I respectfully ask USCIS to let me remain in the United States."
- GOOD: "I beg you to grant me the opportunity to stay legally in the U.S. so that I can seek the help I need, heal, and finally have a dignified life."

**Isolation Integrity:**
- BAD: "Her own daughters called me one night and warned me: 'Leave our mother. She is not a good woman.' My own sister told me the same thing. But I was too broken down and too afraid to leave."
- WHY BAD: Proves victim had people advising him to leave and was not fully isolated. USCIS asks: "Why didn't you leave if people told you to?"
- GOOD: Omit entirely. If needed, reframe: "Even when people around me noticed something was wrong, I could not explain what was happening because of my fear."

**Labor Paragraph Granularity:**
- BAD (compressed): "She forced me to cook dinner every single night. After cooking, I had to wash the dishes, clean the kitchen, scrub the bathrooms, and mop the floors."
- GOOD (separate paragraphs with procedural detail): "Most days, I came home late and tired, and I still had to cook. I would make dinner from scratch — rice, beans, meat or chicken, tortillas, and eggs, depending on what we had. I also had to prepare food for the next day, like packing leftovers or getting things ready so she wouldn't complain in the morning." [separate ¶] "I also had to clean the house constantly. I would sweep and mop the floors, clean the bathrooms, wipe down counters, and pick up the mess. I had to do laundry and fold clothes, and keep the house organized even after being gone all day working."
